The fake Medicare call that's targeting older adults right now.

How to spot it in the first 30 seconds — and exactly what to say before you hang up.

Scam Protection · March 3, 2026 · 3 min read

In this article:

  1. What the call sounds like
  2. Why these scams work so well
  3. The four words that give it away
  4. What to say before you hang up
  5. This week's AI prompt
  6. Scam watch

The call sounds completely official.

A calm, professional voice tells you there has been suspicious activity on your Medicare account. Your benefits are at risk. You need to verify your information right now to keep your coverage active.

It sounds real. It feels urgent. And it is designed to make you act before you think.

What the Call Sounds Like

Medicare phone scams are now the most common financial fraud targeting adults over 60.

According to the FBI, older Americans lost more than $3.4 billion to phone fraud last year alone. Medicare scams account for a significant portion of those losses.

The scammer may claim to be from Medicare, the Social Security Administration, or even your own insurance company.

They use official-sounding language, fake case numbers, and urgent deadlines to create panic — and panic is what they are counting on.

Why These Scams Work So Well

These calls are specifically engineered to target the part of your brain that protects the people you love.

Your healthcare. Your financial security. Your independence. Scammers know these are your most sensitive pressure points.

Many scammers now use AI to clone the voices of real Medicare representatives from recorded calls.

The voice sounds authoritative. The number on your caller ID may even show up as a government number. Neither of these things means the call is real.

The Four Words That Give It Away

Legitimate Medicare representatives will never say these four things on an unsolicited call:

  • "Your benefits will be suspended"
  • "We need to verify your Medicare number"
  • "You must act right now or lose coverage"
  • "Press 1 to speak with a representative"

If you hear any of these phrases — the call is a scam. Full stop.

Medicare does not call you out of the blue to verify information or threaten to suspend your benefits.

What to Say Before You Hang Up

You do not owe the caller anything. You do not need to be polite.

The moment something feels wrong, hang up immediately.

  1. Do not press any numbers or say "yes" to any question
  2. Do not confirm your name, address, date of birth, or Medicare number
  3. Hang up without explaining yourself
  4. Call Medicare directly at 1-800-MEDICARE to verify your account is fine
  5. Report the scam at reportfraud.ftc.gov

If you already gave information to a suspicious caller, do not panic.

Call Medicare immediately at 1-800-MEDICARE and tell them what happened. They will flag your account and guide you through the next steps.

Quick Check

Not sure if a call you received was legitimate? Use this right now:

I just received a call claiming to be from Medicare saying [describe what they said]. They asked me for [what they requested]. Does this sound like a scam? What should I do next?

This Week's Scam Watch

A new version of this scam is now targeting Medicare recipients during open enrollment periods. Scammers call claiming your current plan is being discontinued and you must switch immediately or lose coverage.

Open enrollment has specific dates and Medicare will never call you to force an immediate plan change. If you receive a call like this during any time of year, hang up and call 1-800-MEDICARE directly to check your actual plan status.

You are not gullible for almost falling for one of these calls.

These scams are built by professionals who have studied exactly what to say to make you feel afraid.

Knowing what to listen for is your most powerful protection. Keep this article somewhere you can find it. Share it with someone who needs it.
