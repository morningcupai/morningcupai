Your grandson called. He needed money. But it wasn't him.

How AI voice cloning scams work — and the exact steps that stop them every time.

Scam Protection · January 7, 2026 · 2 min read

In this article:

  1. How voice cloning works
  2. Warning signs to watch for
  3. The family code word defense
  4. What to do if you get a suspicious call
  5. This week's AI prompt
  6. Scam watch

A California mother received a call from her daughter's voice — panicked, crying, saying she had been in a car accident and needed money wired immediately. The voice was perfect. The emotion was real. She sent the money. Then she called her daughter back. Her daughter was at work. She had never been in an accident. The call was AI. This happened last week. And it is happening to families across America every single day.

Voice cloning technology can now copy anyone's voice from just three seconds of audio — pulled from a birthday video on Facebook, an Instagram story, or a voicemail greeting. According to the FBI, Americans lost $893 million to AI-related scams last year, with voice cloning attacks among the fastest growing. A McAfee study found one in four people has encountered an AI voice scam or knows someone who has.

Here is what makes these scams so hard to detect. Scammers often start by breaking into a family member's social media account. They steal voice samples from videos. Then they clone the voice and call you claiming to be that person in an emergency. If you try to verify by calling back through the social media account, the scammer answers — using the cloned voice, from the real account. It looks like your loved one. It sounds like your loved one. It is not your loved one.

The call always comes with urgency. You must act right now. They ask for wire transfers, gift cards, or cash — never anything that can be reversed. They tell you not to tell anyone else in the family. And no matter how real the voice sounds, something feels slightly off — the emotion is there but the conversation is a little too scripted.

The single most effective defense costs nothing and takes two minutes. Create a family code word. Agree on a random word only your family knows — something like pineapple, blue door, or grandma's kitchen. If you get a distressing call from a family member, ask for the code word before doing anything else. A real family member knows it instantly. An AI scammer never will.

The second defense is even simpler. Hang up and call back on the real number you have saved. Do not call back through social media. Do not call the number that called you. Call the number you have always used. If the emergency is real, they will answer. If it was a scam, you just saved yourself everything.

Set up your family code word this week. Text your children and grandchildren right now and agree on the word. Write it down somewhere safe. It takes two minutes and it could save you from losing everything.

This Week's AI Prompt

Open ChatGPT or Claude and paste this in to create a family safety plan you can print and share:

I want to protect my family from AI voice cloning scams. Can you help me create a simple one-page family safety plan that includes: our agreed code word (I will fill this in), the warning signs to watch for on a suspicious call, the exact steps to take if we receive a suspicious call, and a list of real phone numbers for family members to call back directly. Make it simple enough to print and put on the refrigerator.

This Week's Scam Watch

A new variation of the voice cloning scam is targeting older adults through fake Medicare and Social Security calls. Scammers clone the voice of a previous caller or a recorded government voice, then call claiming your benefits are suspended due to suspicious activity. The government will never call you unexpectedly demanding immediate action. If you receive a call like this, hang up and call the official number directly — Medicare at 1-800-MEDICARE or Social Security at 1-800-772-1213.

You are not naive for almost falling for this. These scams are designed by professionals who study human psychology. The California mother who lost thousands is intelligent, caring, and alert — and she was still fooled. Knowing this technology exists is your greatest protection. Set up your code word today.
